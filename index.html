Surgical Counting

Surgical Counts Are a Standard Safety Procedure

In every surgical procedure, items introduced into the operating field must be accounted for. Instruments, needles, sponges, gauze, and other surgical materials are counted before, during, and before closure to help protect patient safety.

One missing item can change everything.

A single counting discrepancy can delay closure, trigger repeated checks, require imaging, create stress for the surgical team, and in rare cases lead to serious patient harm.

Why Counts Matter

Surgical counts help ensure that every item entering the sterile field is accounted for before the procedure is completed.

Why It Gets Difficult

Operating rooms involve fast decisions, instrument exchanges, multiple staff members, emergency changes, fatigue, and high-pressure workflow.

When Counts Do Not Match

Teams may need to recount, inspect the surgical field, check waste areas, review records, and perform additional verification before closure.

Why Technology Helps

SurgiCount is being developed to add an extra layer of visibility, accountability, and real-time support to existing surgical counting practices.

Global Patient Safety Concern

Retained Surgical Items are considered rare, but they continue to be reported worldwide. AHRQ PSNet cites an estimated RSI incidence of about 1.3 per 10,000 surgical procedures. The Joint Commission reported 119 unintended retention events in 2024 among submitted sentinel event reports.

These figures are used for awareness only. Actual rates can vary by reporting system, hospital setting, surgical type, and whether events are internally or externally reported.

RSI Awareness

Understanding Retained Surgical Items

Retained Surgical Items are not just a surgical counting issue. They are a patient safety, workflow, documentation, and accountability challenge inside the operating room.

What Are RSIs?

Retained Surgical Items are surgical sponges, needles, instruments, or other procedural materials unintentionally left inside a patient after surgery.

Why Do They Happen?

Operating rooms are high-pressure environments involving multiple teams, rapid decisions, instrument exchanges, consumables, and repeated verification steps.

Current Prevention Methods

Hospitals rely on manual counts, surgical safety checklists, team verification, documentation, staff training, and imaging when clinically required.

When Counts Do Not Match

Teams may need to recount, inspect the surgical field, check drapes and waste areas, review documentation, and perform additional verification before closure.

Why Accountability Matters

Surgical item accountability supports patient safety, operating room efficiency, quality assurance, documentation, and institutional safety standards.

The Future

Future operating rooms may use computer vision, intelligent sensing, real-time monitoring, and workflow automation to support safer surgical accountability.
